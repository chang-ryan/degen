# Strong data, weak stocks — the healthier of two selloffs

## Link / origin
Verbal — captured from team chat (Inspector Lee), 2026-06-05 12:47 PM. Full text below.

## What it says (the claim)
The market was set up to fall regardless of the jobs print; the only question was which "cope" investors reached for first. The two outcomes were weak jobs (Scenario A) or strong jobs + upward revisions (Scenario B) — and stocks sold off on B, because the market wasn't after good news, it was after **rate cuts**. Healthy payrolls mean the Fed can stay restrictive longer, so the rate-cut bid the 9-week rally leaned on got pulled. His verdict: B is the *healthier* correction — Scenario A (labor genuinely rolling over) stacked on the existing pile (oil near $100, Mideast, private credit, lowest savings rate since 2022, extreme single-stock leverage/vol, ATHs) would have been the genuinely dangerous one. A 5-10% pullback that flushes leverage and cools speculation leaves the market better positioned; meaningful S&P support sits ~7,000.

## Full note (verbatim)
> The funny thing about today is that the market was probably going down no matter what happened. We just needed to see which flavor of cope investors were going to choose first.
>
> There were really only two possible outcomes: Scenario A: jobs come in weak. Scenario B: jobs come in strong, like they did today, with upward revisions. Turns out the market hated both. Which is impressive. It's like ordering a steak, getting exactly the steak you ordered, and then complaining because you were secretly hoping for ice cream.
>
> Regardless of which scenario showed up, investors were still staring at the same list of problems: oil flirting with $100, Middle East tensions, refined product inventories rolling over with no meaningful SPR backstop, the lowest savings rate since 2022, hawkish central banks, the ECB continuing to warn about inflation pressures, private credit fears making another comeback tour, AI threatening to disintermediate entire industries, Anthropic telling the WSJ that AI may start replacing humans, an S&P that has rallied for nine consecutive weeks, markets sitting at all-time highs, levered single-stock ETF exposure approaching levels that would make a casino pit boss uncomfortable, and single-stock volatility ripping higher, with only two observations since the GFC showing worse 12- and 18-month forward volatility readings.
>
> So today we learned something important: the market wasn't looking for good news—it was looking for rate cuts. Bad jobs? Cuts. Weak growth? Cuts. Consumer rolling over? Cuts. Investors were willing to workshop almost any scenario as long as it ended with cuts. Then payrolls showed up looking healthy and everyone suddenly remembered that strong growth and strong employment occasionally lead to central banks doing central bank things.
>
> Apparently the preferred outcome was a labor market that weakened just enough to force cuts, but not enough to hurt earnings—the macro equivalent of wanting to eat Taco Bell four nights a week while somehow getting shredded. Unfortunately, economics tends to ruin these kinds of plans.
>
> Because if you combine all of the existing concerns—geopolitics, inflation, leverage, crowded positioning, AI disruption, and private credit worries—with a genuinely weakening labor market, shrinking savings, and slowing consumer spending, the list of things that can go wrong gets very long very quickly. In other words, Scenario A would've been the genuinely concerning outcome.
> Instead, we got Scenario B. The economy is still growing, people still have jobs, and demand hasn't fallen off a cliff. Stocks sold off because investors realized the Fed may have to stay restrictive for longer to keep inflation from making an unexpected return appearance. That's a healthier correction than the alternative.
>
> The froth was already there. The leverage was already there. The all-time highs were already there. Parts of the market were operating on vibes, caffeine, and 3x leverage. Eventually gravity sends the calendar invite.
>
> Our indicators have been suggesting risk assets were increasingly vulnerable to a pullback, and historically, periods following nine consecutive positive weeks for the S&P have often produced better opportunities after some near-term pain. So a selloff driven by stronger-than-expected economic data isn't necessarily bad news. Strong data. Weak stocks.
>
> A 5-10% pullback that flushes leverage, cools speculative excess, compresses stretched multiples, and improves positioning would probably leave the market in a healthier place. Right now, parts of the market feel like a bachelor party in Vegas that somehow lasted nine straight weeks. Fun while it lasts. Eventually somebody has to look at the credit card statement.
>
> Technically, meaningful support sits around 7,000 on the S&P, with several less significant moving-average support levels between current levels and that zone. If we get there through stronger economic data forcing a reset in expectations, that's a far better outcome than discovering the economy was actually rolling over underneath all the optimism.
